\section{Experimental results and comments}
    \subsection{Running time}
        The four line graphs below illustrate the running times of 12 
        sorting algorithms across four input data distributions: 
        \textbf{sorted, nearly sorted, reverse sorted, and random}.

        \indent Overall, it is evident that \textbf{Selection Sort} and \textbf{Bubble Sort}
        exhibit significantly higher execution times compared to 
        the other algorithms. Notably, \textbf{Counting Sort} remains the 
        most efficient algorithm across all test cases.

        \indent In the sorted and nearly sorted scenarios, \textbf{Selection Sort} 
        and \textbf{Bubble Sort} consistently record the highest running times.
        
        \indent Regarding the reverse sorted and randomized input, five algorithms show a 
        dramatic increase in execution time: \textbf{Insertion Sort}, \textbf{Binary Insertion Sort}, 
        \textbf{Shaker Sort}, and the two previously mentioned algorithms (i.e., \textbf{Selection Sort} and \textbf{Bubble Sort}).
    \subsection{The number of comparisons}
        The four bar charts below display the number of comparisons made by
        12 different categories of sorting algorithms across four input data types:
        \textbf{sorted, nearly sorted, reverse sorted, and random}.

        \indent Generally, it is clear that \textbf{Selection Sort} and \textbf{Bubble Sort} consistently
        require the highest number of comparisons across all scenarios.
        In constrast, \textbf{Counting Sort} exibits the lowest qunatity of comparison count.

        \indent In the sorted and nearly sorted cases, \textbf{Selection Sort} and \textbf{Shaker Sort}
        perform the remarkably well, with their comparion counts being significantly
        lower than the other algorithms. 

        \indent In the reverse sorted and randomized input scenarios,
        the number of comparisons for most $O(n^2)$ algorithms (specifically \textbf{Insertion Sort},
        \textbf{Binary Insertion Sort}, and \textbf{Shaker Sort}) increases dramatically.
        Meanwhile, $O(n \log n)$ algorithms such as \textbf{Merge Sort}, \textbf{Heap Sort}, and \textbf{Quick Sort}
        maintain a relatively stable and efficient number of comparisons.
